Chemical Reactions

A lesson for Klins on how atoms rearrange, why bonds break and form, how energy flows through reactions, and how chemists recognize and describe change.

The central idea: chemical reactions do not create or destroy atoms. They rearrange them. Old bonds break, new bonds form, and the resulting substances have different properties.

1. What a chemical reaction really is

A reaction is a rearrangement of atoms, not a disappearance of matter.

Reactants

The starting substances

Reactants are the materials present before the reaction. Their atoms are already bonded together in particular arrangements.

Bonds

Connections must change

Some existing bonds must break, and new bonds must form. Breaking bonds requires energy; forming bonds releases energy.

Products

New substances appear

Products contain the same atoms as the reactants, but arranged differently. That new arrangement gives them new properties.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

A useful way to picture it

Imagine disassembling two small machines and reusing every part to build two different machines. Nothing vanished. The parts were reorganized. Chemical reactions work the same way, except the parts are atoms and the connections are chemical bonds.

2. Why collisions matter

Atoms and molecules must meet under the right conditions before a reaction can occur.

They must collide

Reacting particles must physically encounter one another. No collision means no opportunity to rearrange bonds.

They need enough energy

A weak collision may not overcome the energy barrier needed to break existing bonds. This minimum barrier is called activation energy.

They need the right orientation

Molecules must strike in a useful arrangement. Even an energetic collision can fail if the reactive parts do not line up properly.

3. Energy changes

Every reaction involves an energy exchange.

Exothermic reactions

Exothermic reactions release more energy when new bonds form than they absorb when old bonds break. The surroundings may become warmer, brighter, or louder.

  • Combustion
  • Rusting
  • Many neutralization reactions
  • Cellular respiration

Endothermic reactions

Endothermic reactions absorb more energy than they release. The surroundings may cool as energy enters the reacting system.

  • Photosynthesis
  • Some decomposition reactions
  • Cold-pack reactions
  • Thermal decomposition

4. Signs that a reaction may be happening

These clues suggest chemical change, though context still matters.

Color change

A new color may indicate that a new substance has formed.

Temperature change

Heating or cooling without an external source can reveal energy release or absorption.

Gas production

Bubbles or fizzing may show that a gaseous product is forming.

Precipitate formation

A solid may appear when two solutions react and produce an insoluble compound.

Light emission

Some reactions release energy as visible light.

Odor change

A new smell may indicate new volatile substances, though smelling chemicals directly is unsafe.

5. Common reaction patterns

These categories describe broad ways atoms rearrange.

Synthesis

A + B → AB

Two or more simpler substances combine to form a more complex product.

Decomposition

AB → A + B

One compound breaks into simpler substances.

Single replacement

A + BC → AC + B

One element replaces another element in a compound.

Double replacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Two compounds exchange components.

Combustion

Fuel + O₂ → products + energy

A fuel reacts with oxygen, often producing carbon dioxide, water, heat, and light.

Redox

Electrons move

Oxidation-reduction reactions involve electron transfer and include batteries, corrosion, and combustion.

6. What changes reaction rate

Reaction speed depends on how often useful collisions occur.

Temperature

Higher temperature usually makes particles move faster, increasing collision frequency and energy.

Concentration

More particles in the same volume means more possible collisions.

Surface area

Breaking a solid into smaller pieces exposes more particles to contact.

Mixing

Stirring brings fresh reactants into contact and reduces stagnant zones.

Pressure

For gases, higher pressure packs particles closer together and increases collision frequency.

Catalysts

Catalysts provide an easier reaction pathway with lower activation energy.

7. Catalysts: changing the route, not the destination

A catalyst speeds a reaction without being consumed overall.

Lower activation energy

A catalyst gives reacting particles a different pathway that requires less initial energy. More collisions become successful, so the reaction proceeds faster.

Examples in ordinary life

  • Enzymes in digestion and metabolism
  • Catalytic converters in vehicles
  • Metal catalysts in fuel cells
  • Industrial catalysts used to make fertilizers and fuels

8. Balancing equations

Balancing is bookkeeping for atoms.

Unbalanced

An unbalanced equation may show the correct substances but the wrong number of atoms on each side.

CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Balanced

Coefficients are added so the number of each type of atom is equal before and after the reaction.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

Why coefficients matter

The small numbers written in front of formulas do not change the identity of a molecule. They tell us how many molecules or moles participate. Changing the small number inside a formula would create a different substance.

9. Chemical reactions in familiar systems

Chemistry is not confined to laboratories.

Batteries

Redox reactions move electrons through an external circuit, producing electrical energy.

Rusting

Iron reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxides.

Cooking

Heat drives reactions that change proteins, sugars, aromas, colors, and textures.

Combustion engines

Fuel reacts with oxygen, releasing energy that produces pressure and motion.

Photosynthesis

Plants use light energy to build glucose from carbon dioxide and water.

Medicine

Drugs participate in or alter chemical processes inside the body.

10. A learning path for Klins

A sequence for turning the poster into working knowledge.

1

Identify reactants and products

Read simple equations and name what exists before and after the arrow.

2

Count atoms

Verify that every element appears in equal quantity on both sides of a balanced equation.

3

Classify the reaction

Decide whether the pattern is synthesis, decomposition, replacement, combustion, or redox.

4

Track energy

Ask whether energy enters or leaves the system and what evidence supports that conclusion.

5

Predict rate changes

Consider how temperature, concentration, surface area, mixing, pressure, or catalysts would affect speed.

6

Connect chemistry to systems

Relate reactions to batteries, corrosion, combustion, metabolism, cleaning, materials, and energy storage.

The larger lesson

Chemistry is the study of controlled rearrangement. Matter changes because atoms can trade partners, release energy, absorb energy, and form new structures. The same principles explain a flame, a battery, rust, digestion, photosynthesis, and the chemistry of stars.

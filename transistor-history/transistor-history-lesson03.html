Poster 3 · December 16, 1947

The First Transistor

A lesson for Klins on the first successful point-contact transistor, the fragile germanium experiment that proved solid-state amplification was possible, and the moment electronics began moving away from vacuum tubes.

The central idea: the first transistor was not important because it was polished or practical. It was important because it worked. It proved that a solid material could amplify a signal without a vacuum, heater, or filament.

1. The moment of discovery

A tiny piece of germanium produced a much larger output from a small input.

Step 1

Prepare the germanium

A small piece of high-purity germanium was mounted and prepared so its electrical behavior could be controlled and measured.

Step 2

Place the contacts

Two closely spaced gold point contacts were pressed against the semiconductor surface, along with a base connection to the crystal.

Step 3

Apply a small input

A small electrical signal at one contact changed the current collected at another.

Step 4

Observe amplification

The output signal was larger than the input. That was the decisive evidence that the solid-state device could amplify.

small input signal → changed carrier behavior in germanium → larger output signal

The breakthrough was proof

The device was delicate, irregular, and difficult to reproduce. But once amplification appeared, the central question had been answered: a solid could perform the essential job of a vacuum tube.

2. How the first point-contact transistor worked

The device used closely spaced contacts to control current through a semiconductor.

Emitter point

Injecting carriers

One gold contact injected charge carriers into the germanium near the surface.

Base

The semiconductor body

The germanium crystal provided the region through which the carrier concentration and current could be altered.

Collector point

Collecting the controlled current

The second gold contact collected a current influenced by the input at the first contact.

3. Why this counts as amplification

A small control action changed a larger output.

The control signal

The input did not directly provide all the output energy. It changed the device conditions that governed a larger current supplied by the circuit.

The larger result

The collector output reproduced the shape of the input while producing greater signal power. That is the essential function of an amplifier.

4. The team behind the first transistor

Different talents converged on one difficult problem.

William Shockley

Led the broader solid-state research effort and pursued the theoretical possibility of a semiconductor amplifier.

John Bardeen

Developed crucial insight into semiconductor surfaces and why expected field effects were being blocked.

Walter Brattain

Built and adjusted the experimental device, using exceptional laboratory skill to make the effect measurable.

The discovery was both theoretical and physical

The transistor required an explanation of what electrons were doing, but it also required someone to shape contacts, prepare materials, adjust geometry, and recognize a real signal when it appeared.

5. Not perfect—but unmistakably a breakthrough

The first device proved the principle without solving every engineering problem.

Fragile

The point contacts had to be placed precisely and could be disturbed easily.

Irregular

Performance varied from device to device because materials and surfaces were not yet controlled well enough.

Limited operating range

The device worked only under certain current, contact, and bias conditions.

Difficult to manufacture

Point contacts were poorly suited to mass production compared with later junction designs.

Still revolutionary

It needed no vacuum, filament, or warm-up time and consumed far less power.

A platform for better designs

Once the principle was proven, researchers could pursue junction transistors, improved materials, and more reliable manufacturing.

6. One small device, endless impact

The point-contact transistor opened the route to an entire electronic ecosystem.

Smaller electronics

Solid-state devices could replace bulky tubes and reduce the size of radios, amplifiers, and control systems.

Lower power

No heater filament meant far less wasted energy and less heat.

Greater reliability

Solid devices had no fragile glass envelope or hot filament to burn out.

Integrated circuits

Transistor functions could eventually be manufactured together on one piece of semiconductor.

Computers

Millions and later billions of solid-state switches made compact digital computation possible.

Communication everywhere

Radios, satellites, phones, networks, and embedded systems all descend from the same basic breakthrough.

7. Connecting the first transistor to your bench

The historic point-contact device and your NPN transistor perform the same broad kind of control.

Small input, larger output

On your bench, a small base current allows a larger collector current to operate an LED, fan, buzzer, or relay coil.

Solid-state switching

The transistor replaces a mechanical contact with controlled carrier motion inside a semiconductor.

From germanium to silicon

Your modern NPN transistor is usually silicon-based, more stable, more repeatable, and easier to manufacture than the first point-contact device.

Raspberry Pi GPIO control

A GPIO pin provides a small control signal; the transistor handles a load the GPIO should not drive directly.

Relay driver connection

Your transistor-and-relay experiments reproduce the same system logic: a small electrical command controls a larger separate circuit.

Measurement closes the loop

Measuring base voltage, collector current, and load behavior lets you observe the same amplification principle that Bell Labs first proved.

point-contact transistor → junction transistor → integrated circuit → microcontroller → Raspberry Pi / ESP32 projects

8. The road to December 1947

The first transistor grew from decades of earlier breakthroughs.

1904

Fleming valve

The vacuum diode demonstrates controlled electron flow in an evacuated tube.

1906

Audion triode

Lee de Forest adds a control grid and makes electronic amplification practical.

1920s–1930s

Vacuum-tube electronics expand

Radio, telephony, radar research, and early electronics establish the need for better amplifiers.

1930s

Quantum theory explains solids

Energy-band and carrier models improve understanding of semiconductor behavior.

1940s

Purified germanium and better measurement

Improved materials and test methods make delicate semiconductor effects observable.

1945

Bell Labs forms a focused group

Shockley organizes a team to search for a practical solid-state amplifier.

December 16, 1947

The first working transistor

Bardeen and Brattain demonstrate amplification with a point-contact germanium device.

The larger lesson for Klins

The first transistor mattered because it changed the status of an idea. Solid-state amplification was no longer a hope or theory—it was an observed fact. From that moment, the engineering problem became how to make the device better, smaller, repeatable, and manufacturable.

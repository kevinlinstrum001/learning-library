Course purpose

Learn the language beneath science and engineering

Mathematical Foundations develops the concepts needed to express measurements, motion, relationships, rates of change, and spatial fields. Rather than treating mathematics as a collection of disconnected procedures, the course builds a continuous path from simple quantities to the operators used in Maxwell's equations.

The course begins with scalars and vectors, then develops components, vector operations, matrices, and functions. It continues through limits, derivatives, integrals, and partial derivatives before introducing vector fields, divergence, and curl.

Starting point

No formal prerequisite is required. The course begins with the basic idea of a measurable quantity.

Destination

You will be prepared to understand the mathematical structure used in mechanics, electricity, magnetism, and electromagnetism.

Skills gained

What you will be able to do

By the end of the course, you will be able to interpret and use the mathematical objects that describe physical systems.

Describe quantities

Distinguish scalars from vectors and represent vectors using components and unit vectors.

Operate on vectors

Add vectors, scale them, and use dot and cross products to describe geometric and physical relationships.

Use transformations

Interpret matrices as structured operations that transform vectors and organize systems of equations.

Model relationships

Understand functions as mappings between inputs and outputs and use them to describe systems.

Analyze change

Use limits, derivatives, integrals, and partial derivatives to study rates, accumulation, and multivariable behavior.

Understand fields

Interpret vector fields and the meanings of divergence and curl in physical space.

Next course connection

Course 2 applies the mathematics to motion

Classical Mechanics uses the language developed here to describe position, velocity, acceleration, force, work, energy, momentum, and rotation.

Why this course comes first

Mechanics immediately depends on vectors and derivatives: position is represented with coordinates, velocity is the rate of change of position, acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, and forces combine through vector addition. The later courses on electricity and magnetism then extend those same mathematical ideas to electric and magnetic fields.

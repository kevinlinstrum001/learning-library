Vectors

A vector describes both how much and which way. Vectors are the mathematical language of displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, electric fields, magnetic fields, and many other directional quantities.

Prerequisite: scalars Core idea: magnitude + direction Next: vector components

Lesson contents

  1. What a vector is
  2. How vectors are represented
  3. How to interpret vectors
  4. Check your understanding

Section 1

A vector tells us how much and which way

A scalar gives magnitude only. A vector adds direction, allowing mathematics to describe motion, forces, fields, and changes that point through space.

Vector: a quantity with both magnitude and direction.

The statements 5 meters and 5 meters east do not describe the same kind of quantity. The first is a scalar distance. The second is a vector displacement because it includes a size and a direction.

Scalar

  • Magnitude only
  • Example: 12 seconds
  • Example: 8 kilograms
  • Example: 20 meters of distance

Vector

  • Magnitude and direction
  • Example: 6 meters north
  • Example: 15 newtons downward
  • Example: 20 meters east of the start

Worked example: scalar or vector?

  1. 40 kilometers — scalar distance.
  2. 40 kilometers west — vector displacement.
  3. 12 meters per second — scalar speed.
  4. 12 meters per second upward — vector velocity.

Section summary

A vector is not merely a number with a unit. Its direction is an essential part of the quantity.

Section 2

Vectors are represented by arrows

An arrow gives a vector a visible form: its length represents magnitude, and its arrowhead shows direction.

Vectors are often written with bold symbols such as v, or with an arrow over the symbol, such as v⃗. The magnitude of a vector is written with vertical bars, such as |v⃗|.

Worked example: reading a vector arrow

Suppose a diagram uses a scale of 1 centimeter = 2 newtons. An arrow is 4 centimeters long and points left.

magnitude = 4 cm × 2 N/cm = 8 N

The vector represents a force of 8 newtons to the left.

Section summary

A vector arrow encodes both parts of the quantity: length for magnitude and orientation for direction.

Section 3

Vectors can be compared and interpreted

Two vectors are equal when they have the same magnitude and the same direction, even if they are drawn in different places.

Equal vectors

If two arrows have the same length and point the same way, they represent equal vectors. Their position on the page does not matter unless the problem specifically concerns a fixed point of application.

Opposite vectors

Two vectors with equal magnitude but opposite directions are negatives of one another.

if a⃗ points east, then −a⃗ points west with the same magnitude

Worked example: equal or opposite?

Vector a⃗ is 7 meters north. Vector b⃗ is also 7 meters north. Vector c⃗ is 7 meters south.

  • a⃗ = b⃗ because their magnitudes and directions match.
  • c⃗ = −a⃗ because it has the same magnitude but the opposite direction.

A preview of components

Directions do not always line up exactly with north, south, east, or west. In the next lesson, a vector will be separated into horizontal and vertical components so that angled vectors can be calculated precisely.

Section summary

Equal vectors share magnitude and direction. Opposite vectors share magnitude but point in opposite directions.

Section 4

Check your understanding

Before moving to components, make sure you can identify vectors, describe their arrows, and compare their magnitude and direction.

Quick check

  1. Is 18 kilograms a scalar or vector?
  2. Is 9 meters per second east a scalar or vector?
  3. What feature of a vector arrow represents magnitude?
  4. What feature represents direction?
  5. Are two 5-newton arrows equal if one points north and the other points south?
  6. What vector is opposite to 12 meters west?
Show answers
  1. Scalar.
  2. Vector.
  3. The arrow’s length, interpreted using the diagram’s scale.
  4. The arrow’s orientation and arrowhead.
  5. No. Their magnitudes match, but their directions are opposite.
  6. 12 meters east.

Lesson summary

  • A vector has magnitude and direction.
  • Vector arrows use length to show magnitude and an arrowhead to show direction.
  • Two vectors are equal only when both magnitude and direction match.
  • Opposite vectors have equal magnitudes and opposite directions.
  • Vector components will provide a numerical way to describe angled vectors.

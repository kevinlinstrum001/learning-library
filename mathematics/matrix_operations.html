Introduction to Matrices

Matrices began as a practical way to organize coefficients. This lesson follows their evolution from systems of equations to mappings, patterns, composition, inverses, and modern applications.

Feeding Inputs into a Matrix

H

Historical motivation

Once coefficients were organized, the same system could be evaluated repeatedly for different measurements or observations.

Central idea

A matrix describes how an input list is converted into an output list.

Worked example

\[\begin{bmatrix}3 & 2\\1 & 4\end{bmatrix}\]

Input: \((2, 5)\)

\(u = 3(2) + 2(5) = 16\)
\(v = 1(2) + 4(5) = 22\)

Output: \((16, 22)\)

Recognizing Special Patterns

H

Historical motivation

Repeated calculations revealed that certain coefficient arrangements always produced recognizable behavior.

Central idea

Zeros, diagonals, and off-diagonal entries reveal the structure of a mapping.

Identity

\[\begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 1\end{bmatrix}\]

Behavior: (x, y) -> (x, y)

Swap

\[\begin{bmatrix}0 & 1\\1 & 0\end{bmatrix}\]

Behavior: (x, y) -> (y, x)

Scaling

\[\begin{bmatrix}2 & 0\\0 & 3\end{bmatrix}\]

Behavior: (x, y) -> (2x, 3y)

Chapter 10

Identity, Inverses, and Lost Information

H

Historical motivation

Solving equations often means reversing a calculation to recover unknown inputs.

Central idea

An inverse matrix undoes a mapping. A matrix has no inverse when the original mapping loses information.

Example

Mapping: (x, y) -> (2x, 3y)

Inverse: (u, v) -> (u/2, v/3)

Example

Mapping: (x, y) -> (x, 0)

Inverse: none

The original y value was discarded.

Chapter 12

Modern Applications and Final Investigation

H

Historical motivation

Matrices became central wherever many quantities interact: engineering, economics, computer graphics, statistics, quantum mechanics, networks, and machine learning.

Central idea

The arrangement of entries can reveal structure in a real system.

friction coefficient tablesroad-distance matriceselectrical networksprobability transition matricesimage-processing kernelscomputer graphics transformations
Final investigation
  1. What do the rows represent?
  2. What do the columns represent?
  3. What does each entry mean?
  4. Where are the zeros and what do they signify?
  5. Are there diagonal, symmetric, repeated, or sparse patterns?
  6. Is the matrix storing data, acting as a transformation, or both?
  7. What does its pattern reveal about the underlying system?

Derivative Lab

Chapter One: a careful investigation of slope, rate of change, secant lines, tangent lines, and the derivative of \(x^2\) from first principles.

CH01 · CALCULUS
System: Differential Calculus Function: \(f(x)=x^2\) Method: Limit Definition Revision: 01

The First Moving Mechanism

The assistant and the wizard's Siamese cat enter a chamber crossed by a narrow stone bridge. At the far side, an iron gate rises according to a glowing rule carved into the wall:

\[ H(t)=t^2 \]

The assistant can calculate the gate's height at any moment. That is not enough. To cross safely, he must know how quickly the gate is moving at a particular instant.

The Assistant “The equation tells me where the gate is. It does not tell me whether it is creeping upward or falling fast enough to crush me.”
The Cat “Then stop asking only where it is.”

Determine the derivative of \(f(x)=x^2\)

Use the limit definition rather than the memorized power rule. The objective is not merely to obtain \(2x\), but to understand where it comes from.

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]

Lesson Objectives

  • Distinguish position from rate of change.
  • Calculate an average rate of change over an interval.
  • Interpret secant-line slope as average rate of change.
  • Interpret tangent-line slope as instantaneous rate of change.
  • Explain the role of \(h\) in the difference quotient.
  • Derive \(f'(x)=2x\) from first principles.
  • Evaluate the derivative at a specific point.
01

Position is not motion

A function can describe the state of a mechanism. If the gate height is \(H(t)=t^2\), then the input \(t\) is time and the output \(H(t)\) is height.

At \(t=2\)
\[ H(2)=2^2=4 \]

The gate is four units above its starting position.

At \(t=3\)
\[ H(3)=3^2=9 \]

The gate is nine units above its starting position.

A

The assistant's mistake: he initially assumes that \(H(3)=9\) means the gate is moving at nine units per second. The cat points out that height and velocity are different kinds of information.

02

Average rate of change

Over an interval, the average rate of change is the change in output divided by the change in input.

Average Rate of Change

Between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\):

\[ \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \]

For \(H(t)=t^2\), between \(t=2\) and \(t=3\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{H(3)-H(2)}{3-2} &= \frac{9-4}{1} \\[6pt] &= 5 \end{aligned} \]
The gate rises an average of five units per unit of time during this interval.

But between \(t=3\) and \(t=4\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{H(4)-H(3)}{4-3} &= \frac{16-9}{1} \\[6pt] &= 7 \end{aligned} \]
The two nearby intervals give different average rates. Neither one is automatically the exact rate at \(t=3\).
03

Narrow the interval

To estimate the instantaneous rate at \(t=3\), choose a second time closer and closer to \(3\).

Starting time Nearby time Average rate
\(3\) \(4\) \(7\)
\(3\) \(3.5\) \(6.5\)
\(3\) \(3.1\) \(6.1\)
\(3\) \(3.01\) \(6.01\)
\(3\) \(3.001\) \(6.001\)

The average rates approach \(6\). This suggests that the instantaneous rate at \(t=3\) is:

\[ H'(3)=6 \]
04

Introduce the nearby input \(x+h\)

Instead of repeatedly choosing decimal values, represent a nearby input symbolically.

Original input
\[ x \]
Nearby input
\[ x+h \]

The symbol \(h\) represents the distance between the two input values.

Change in output
\[ f(x+h)-f(x) \]

The change in input is:

\[ (x+h)-x=h \]

Therefore, the average rate over an interval of width \(h\) is:

\[ \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]
C

The cat's warning: \(f(x+h)\) does not mean \(f(x)+h\). The entire new input \(x+h\) must be substituted into the function.

05

The limit definition of the derivative

The derivative is the value approached by the average rate of change as the interval width \(h\) approaches zero.

Derivative from First Principles

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]
The limit does not instruct us to divide by zero. It asks what value the expression approaches while \(h\) remains nonzero but becomes arbitrarily small.
06

Substitute \(f(x)=x^2\)

Begin with the definition:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]

Because \(f(x)=x^2\), the shifted function value is:

\[ f(x+h)=(x+h)^2 \]

Substitute both function values:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h} \]
07

Expand the squared binomial

Multiply \((x+h)\) by itself:

\[ \begin{aligned} (x+h)^2 &= (x+h)(x+h) \\[6pt] &= x^2+xh+hx+h^2 \\[6pt] &= x^2+2xh+h^2 \end{aligned} \]

Replace the squared binomial in the difference quotient:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{x^2+2xh+h^2-x^2}{h} \]
08

Cancel and factor

The \(+x^2\) and \(-x^2\) terms cancel:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{2xh+h^2}{h} \]

Factor \(h\) from the numerator:

\[ 2xh+h^2 = h(2x+h) \]

Substitute the factored form:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} \frac{h(2x+h)}{h} \]

For nonzero \(h\), cancel the common factor:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0} (2x+h) \]
The expression is now simplified. The division by \(h\) that caused the indeterminate form has disappeared.
09

Evaluate the limit

As \(h\) approaches zero, the expression \(2x+h\) approaches \(2x\).

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \lim_{h\to0} (2x+h) \\[7pt] &= 2x+0 \\[7pt] &= 2x \end{aligned} \]
\[ \boxed{ \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)=2x } \]

Return to the Gate

The derivative is a function. It gives the gate's instantaneous rate of change at every time \(t\):

\[ H'(t)=2t \]

At \(t=3\):

\[ H'(3)=2(3)=6 \]

The gate is moving at six units per unit of time at that instant.

10

Read the derivative as a new function

The original function and derivative answer different questions.

Time Height \(H(t)=t^2\) Velocity \(H'(t)=2t\)
\(0\) \(0\) \(0\)
\(1\) \(1\) \(2\)
\(2\) \(4\) \(4\)
\(3\) \(9\) \(6\)
\(4\) \(16\) \(8\)
C

The cat steps through the gate without waiting. The assistant remains behind long enough to copy both functions onto his slate. Knowing the gate's height was useful. Knowing its rate of change was the key.

Confirmed Derivative \[ \boxed{ \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)=2x } \]

The derivative is obtained by comparing two nearby function values, dividing by the width of the interval, simplifying the difference quotient, and then examining the limiting value as the interval shrinks toward zero.

Independent Practice

Use the same limit-definition process to differentiate:

\[ f(x)=x^2+3 \]
Reveal the worked solution
\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \lim_{h\to0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \\[7pt] &= \lim_{h\to0} \frac{(x+h)^2+3-(x^2+3)}{h} \\[7pt] &= \lim_{h\to0} \frac{x^2+2xh+h^2+3-x^2-3}{h} \\[7pt] &= \lim_{h\to0} \frac{2xh+h^2}{h} \\[7pt] &= \lim_{h\to0} (2x+h) \\[7pt] &= 2x \end{aligned} \]

The added constant does not affect the derivative because its rate of change is zero.

Concept check: why does the constant disappear?

Adding \(3\) shifts every output upward by the same amount. It does not change how rapidly the outputs vary as \(x\) changes. Therefore:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(3)=0 \]

# Course 6 — Systems, Operations, and Control

## Course purpose

Explain how complex human and technical systems convert intent into coordinated action, verify execution, respond to feedback, and manage exceptions.

## Intended audience

Learners interested in operations, engineering, software, control systems, safety-critical work, and organizational design.

## Prerequisites

None for the conceptual lessons.

Course 1 becomes useful when the course reaches mathematical control theory, signals, or quantitative feedback analysis.

## Course approach

The course begins with human and organizational systems, then connects those ideas to machines, software, and formal control systems.

Examples will include:

- naval operations;
- hospital emergency response;
- utilities and infrastructure;
- industrial operations;
- software systems;
- automated control.

## Core model

1. Intent
2. Procedure
3. Execution
4. Verification
5. Physical feedback
6. Automatic response

## Lesson sequence

### Lesson 1 — Systems, Boundaries, and Responsibilities

Define a system, its purpose, its boundary, its environment, and the responsibilities assigned to its parts.

### Lesson 2 — Intent, Procedure, and Execution

Explain how high-level intent becomes an authorized procedure and then distributed action.

### Lesson 3 — Verification and Closed-Loop Reporting

Explain acknowledgment, completion reports, status tracking, discrepancies, and confirmation of actual results.

### Lesson 4 — State, Conditions, and Configuration

Explain operating states, readiness conditions, configuration control, and the difference between declared state and actual state.

### Lesson 5 — Sensors and Physical Feedback

Explain how systems observe physical reality and return information to operators and controllers.

### Lesson 6 — Automatic Response and Control

Explain automatic reactions, controllers, limits, interlocks, and human supervision.

### Lesson 7 — Exceptions, Overrides, and Modified Conditions

Explain authorized deviations, temporary exceptions, degraded modes, overrides, and restoration to normal configuration.

### Lesson 8 — Distributed Coordination

Explain how multiple departments, stations, services, or software components coordinate without one person performing every action.

### Lesson 9 — Human Operators in Safety-Critical Systems

Explain qualification, watchstanding, decision authority, workload, communication, and human-machine boundaries.

### Lesson 10 — Comparative Case Studies

Compare ships, hospitals, utilities, industrial systems, and software using the same layered model.

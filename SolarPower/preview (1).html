Proposed 12‑Volt Yard Solar Power Setup

A small, expandable off-grid system centered on one 200-watt rigid solar panel, a 30-amp MPPT controller, a 12.8-volt 100Ah LiFePO₄ battery, and a 700-watt pure sine-wave inverter.

Concept Layout Equipment Wiring Energy budget Build sequence Safety

System concept

The first version is intended for learning, backup power, lighting, charging electronics, communications equipment, and other modest loads. It is not intended to power the entire house.

Yard-to-house layout

The panel remains outdoors on a secure ground mount. The controller, battery, fuse, disconnect, battery monitor, and inverter remain together in a dry equipment area near the cable entry point.

Protection and wiring plan

The final wire gauges and fuse ratings depend on actual cable length, conductor temperature rating, installation method, and manufacturer instructions.

Connection Protection and design intent Planning note
Panel → controller UV-resistant PV wire, weather-rated connectors, cable support, and a PV disconnect. Keep polarity consistent and stay below the controller’s maximum PV voltage.
Controller → battery Short copper conductors with a battery-side fuse or breaker sized for the controller circuit. Renogy specifies an external fuse; the controller does not contain the required battery-circuit fuse.
Battery → inverter Very short, heavy copper cables, a main fuse close to the positive terminal, and a DC-rated disconnect. This is the highest-current branch in the system.
Battery monitor Install the shunt in the battery negative path so all charging and load current passes through it. Only the battery side of the shunt should connect directly to the negative terminal.
High-current caution: At 700W output, the inverter can draw roughly 60A or more from a 12V battery after losses. Follow the inverter manual for cable and fuse sizing rather than choosing by appearance or normal load current alone.

Energy budget

The battery stores approximately 12.8V × 100Ah ≈ 1,280Wh nominally. The usable AC energy will be lower after inverter losses and the reserve you choose to leave in the battery.

A 200W panel does not produce 200W continuously throughout the day. For early planning, a reasonable good-day estimate is roughly 500–800Wh of usable daily solar production, depending on season, tilt, shade, temperature, weather, wiring, and controller efficiency.

Build sequence

1 — Measure
Measure panel-to-controller and battery-to-inverter cable lengths before selecting wire and protection.
2 — Mount
Install and anchor the panel frame. Avoid shade, impact, standing water, and accidental contact.
3 — Assemble
Mount the controller, battery, fuses, disconnects, monitor, and inverter on a durable surface or in a suitable enclosure.
4 — Connect
With PV isolated, connect the battery to the controller first. Then configure battery type and connect the panel. Confirm the sequence in the current manual.
5 — Test
Verify polarity, charging, terminal tightness, voltage, monitor readings, and temperature before applying larger loads.

Safety boundaries

  • Do not connect the inverter to house wiring or an existing receptacle through a backfeed cord.
  • Use only DC-rated fuses, breakers, switches, connectors, and disconnects at the required voltage and current.
  • Place the main battery fuse close to the positive terminal.
  • Protect each conductor according to its ampacity and the connected equipment.
  • Keep the battery within its permitted charging temperature range; the selected battery includes low-temperature charging protection.
  • Confirm grounding, bonding, outdoor wiring, trenching, structural mounting, and local code requirements with a qualified installer or authority.
  • Recheck terminal torque and inspect periodically for heat, corrosion, abrasion, looseness, and water entry.

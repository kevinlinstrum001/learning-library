Proposed 12‑Volt Yard Solar Power System

A small, expandable off-grid system built around one 200-watt solar panel, a 12-volt 100Ah lithium battery, an MPPT charge controller, and a modest pure sine-wave inverter.

System concept

The first version is intended for learning, backup power, lights, charging electronics, and other modest loads—not whole-house operation. The design keeps the battery-side equipment indoors in a dry, ventilated utility area while the panel sits on a secure yard mount.

Inverter recommendation

Start with a 300–600W pure sine-wave inverter. A 600W unit is large enough for chargers, lights, a laptop, a television, and some small tools, but it is still modest enough for a single 100Ah battery.

Load Likely suitability Planning note
Phones, USB chargers, LED lighting Excellent Prefer direct 12V/USB loads where practical to avoid inverter loss.
Laptop, monitor, small television Good Check nameplate watts and startup behavior.
Small fan, router, modem Good Useful backup loads, provided combined watts remain within limits.
Microwave, space heater, hot plate, large refrigerator Poor fit These loads quickly exceed the intended power and energy budget.
Provisional electrical design: A 600W inverter may draw roughly 50–60A from a 12V battery at full load. Cable gauge, fuse size, lug type, run length, and disconnect rating must follow the selected inverter’s manual and applicable electrical rules.

Protection, wiring, and monitoring

PV-side protection

Weather-rated connectors, UV-resistant PV wire, cable support, a readily accessible PV disconnect, and overcurrent protection where required by the final array configuration.

Battery-side protection

A main fuse placed close to the positive battery terminal, a DC-rated disconnect, insulated busbars if branching is needed, and covered terminals.

Monitoring

A shunt-based battery monitor is preferable for state-of-charge tracking because it measures current entering and leaving the battery. Bluetooth controller data is still useful for solar production.

Build sequence

Phase 1 — Design
Choose the inverter, list actual appliances and wattages, measure all cable runs, select the exact panel model, and verify every component’s current and voltage limits.
Phase 2 — Mount
Install and anchor the panel frame. Keep the module clear of shade, impact, standing water, and accidental contact. Plan tilt and orientation for the site.
Phase 3 — Protection
Mount controller, battery, inverter, fuses, disconnects, and monitor on a durable backboard or in an appropriate enclosure. Keep battery cables short.
Phase 4 — Commission
With the panel isolated, connect the controller to the battery first so the controller recognizes system voltage. Then connect the PV input. Confirm the exact sequence in the controller manual.
Phase 5 — Test
Verify polarity and tightness, confirm charging, test a small load, monitor cable and terminal temperature, and record baseline readings before adding larger loads.

Expected first-stage performance

A 200W panel rarely delivers its nameplate rating all day. A reasonable planning estimate is roughly 500–800Wh of usable solar energy on a good day, depending on season, tilt, temperature, shade, wiring, controller efficiency, and local weather. The battery’s nominal storage is about 1,280Wh, but practical AC energy will be lower after reserve margin and inverter losses.

This system is best treated as a small energy budget. Record each load’s watts and hours of use: watt-hours = watts × hours. The daily total should remain comfortably below expected daily solar production.

Safety boundaries

  • Do not connect this inverter to house wiring, an existing receptacle, or the utility panel through a “backfeed” cord.
  • Use only DC-rated fuses, breakers, switches, and connectors at the relevant voltage and fault current.
  • Protect every positive conductor according to its wire ampacity and connected equipment—not merely the normal operating current.
  • Keep lithium batteries within their permitted charging temperature range. Low-temperature charge cutoff depends on the exact battery model.
  • Confirm grounding, bonding, outdoor wiring methods, trenching, structural mounting, and code requirements with a qualified installer or local authority.
  • Recheck torque after initial operation and periodically inspect for heat discoloration, corrosion, abrasion, looseness, and water entry.
